Revisiting Accessibility for 2017 Section 508 Compliance

In 2017, the U.S. Governement revised the IT Accessibility Laws and Policies requirements for section 508 Accessibily which covers web browser based pages and applications.

In this final rule, the Access Board is updating its existing Electronic and Information Technology Accessibility Standards under section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, ("508 Standards"), as well as our Telecommunications Act Accessibility Guidelines under Section 255 of the Communications Act of 1934 ("255 Guidelines"). Given the passage of nearly two decades since their issuance, the existing 508 Standards and 255 Guidelines are in need of a "refresh" in several important respects. This final rule is intended to, among other things, address advances in information and communication technology that have occurred since the guidelines and standards were issued in 1998 and 2000 respectively, harmonize with accessibility standards developed by standards organizations worldwide in recent years, and ensure consistency with the Board's regulations that have been promulgated since the late 1990s. The Revised 508 Standards and 255 Guidelines support the access needs of individuals with disabilities, while also taking into account the costs of providing accessible information and communication technology to Federal agencies, as well as manufacturers of telecommunications equipment and customer premises equipment.
ref: Information and Communication Technology (ICT) Standards and Guidelines

The final rules governing the 508 Standards became effecting January 18, 2019 after more than a decade of discussion and public debate. Government agencies such as the Department of Veterans are covered by the 508 Standards. Failure to comply can lead to significant penalties, ref: reportedly with fines up to $55,000 for the first violation and $110,000 for each subsequent violation".

For web developers (as opposed to, for example, elevator builders), the key requirement is that someone who is disabled can use the product. This can be done using the normal pages for the application or an alternate interface. The 508 Standards define Accessibility: "In general, an information technology system is accessible to people with disabilities if it can be used in a variety of ways that do not depend on a single sense or ability". The key requirements for web builders are to enable blind users to navigate the page and effectively use the screen reader.

The defacto standard screen reader technology in use today is JAWS, a paid prodcut from Freedom scientific. Prior to 2019, JAWS had problems with most browsers except Firefox, so many blind users have adopted JAWS with Firefox. In 2018, however, Firefox introduced the Quantum-based browser, and this version would not work with older versions of JAWS. In 2019, JAWS was updated to work with Firefox Quantum. Thus, in 2019 the prefered screen reader configuration is generally Microsoft Windows with JAWS 2019 and Firefox ESR 62+ (Quantum). Internet Explorer 11 is often also required as some organizations prevent introduction of new software including browsers; however, Microsoft has announced that Internet Explorer is adopting webkit so it is likely that the JAWS implementation for Internet Explorer will change.

The final rule specifies that web sites and applications must follow the WCAG 2.0 standard from w3.org. Specifically the keyboard accessibility protocols of the WAI-ARIA, the Accessible Rich Internet Applications suite of web standards (ARIA) must be provided, including maintaining a sensible tab order, adding section tags for navigation, and specifying "aria" tags and properties on the web page elements.

Implementers would follow practices of proper ARIA Usage including: 

ARIA Roles: <li role="menuitem">Open file... </l>
ARIA States and Properties: <li role="menuitemcheckbox" aria-checked="true">Sort by Last Modified</li>
ARIA Styles: [aria-checked="true"] { font-weight: bold; }
			 [aria-checked="true"]::before { background-image: url(checked.gif); }
Many websites such as Mozilla, SitePoint and Google offer tips and examples of enabling accessiblity using WP-ARIA.

With the laws in place from 2017 governing all U.S. Government software, and with similar requirements adoped by many other governments and corporations, and because it is the right thing to do, the requirement to implement accessibility protocols is imperative for all major software endeavors.
